You should drive a crappy car. And here's why.

September 14, 2014

In July 2013 I got rid of my gas guzzling pickup truck and bought a 1998 Toyota Corolla for $1,000. This wasn't just any old Corolla, though. The previous owner wrecked it twice before he sold it to me, and had some, uhh, creative, ways of fixing it.

The hood is mangled and must be tied down with a tow strap in order to prevent it from flying up when you drive. The headlights are covered by transparent packing tape and are held in place by a combination of zip ties and duct tape.

The back passenger door is a different color than the rest of the body and is held in place by caulk, a U-bolt, and, inexplicably, a heavy duty padlock that appears to serve no purpose whatsoever. I would cut off the padlock, but I'm afraid to disrupt the delicate balance that is this car's structural integrity.

You'll save money.

Despite its cosmetic flaws, this car is the most reliable vehicle I've ever had the pleasure of owning. Apart from replacing the brakes when I bought it and regular oil changes, which I do myself, I haven't spent a dime on maintenance. The 1.8 liter engine gets 31 miles per gallon on average, and I've gotten almost 40 mpg on long highway road trips. It costs next to nothing to insure and I have no car payment. It's awesome.

Other drivers get out of your way.

Driving around in a car like this, one always has the right of way. If I need to merge into heavy traffic, change lanes, or perform any other maneuver that's typically a pain, the state of my car gives me a free pass to go right in. I just find the nearest BMW, Escalade, or similar yuppie car and go in front of them. When they see my monstrosity barreling down on them, they get out of the way at all costs. Now, in my 11 years of driving, I have an impeccable driving record and have never been in an at fault accident. Other drivers don't know that, though. Thanks to the mistakes of my car's previous owner, other drivers assume that I'm some sort of demolition derby wannabe and give me my own personal buffer zone in traffic. It's like having the road all to myself.

It's very low stress.

Owning a car like this is a particularly low stress endeavor. I can park in the tightest parking spots, because if someone hits my car with their door, the scratch will just blend in with the others. When I finish a cup of coffee on a long road trip, I can just throw the cup on the floor and toss it next time I stop. If someone rear ends me at less than 30 MPH, I can just flip them the bird and keep driving. By contrast, I have a friend who once took on a huge amount of debt to get himself an Audi. He used to drive around the mall parking lot looking for the perfect spot with no adjacent cars. He spent hours washing and waxing his car every weekend. One time I dropped a french fry on the floor by accident and he almost had an aneurysm. After all that, some guy in a $500 minivan ran a red light and T-boned him, totaling his car. The check the insurance company gave him was barely enough to pay the bank what he still owed them on the car.

So, if you like saving money, always having the right of way, and low stress, the best recommendation I can give you is to go out and get a crappy car today.
